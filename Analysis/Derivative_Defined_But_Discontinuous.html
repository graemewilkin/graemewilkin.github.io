A function whose derivative is defined, but discontinuous

Below is the graph of the function \(f(x) = x^2 \sin \left( \frac{1}{x} \right) \).


The slope of the red line is \(\frac{f(x) - f(0)}{x-0} = \frac{f(x)}{x} = x \sin \left( \frac{1}{x} \right)\). By clicking and dragging the point \(A\) you can see how the slope changes. In particular, note how the slope converges to zero as \(A\) gets closer to the origin, which illustrates that \(f'(0) = 0\).

The point \(B\) is drawn with its tangent line. As you move \(B\) towards the origin, the tangent line at \(B\) oscillates wildly, and hence the derivative of the function \(f\) also oscillates wildly. This illustrates that the derivative is discontinuous, since \(\lim_{x \to 0} f'(x)\) does not exist.
