An example of the Intermediate Value Theorem

Back to Calculus homepage

The following picture shows the graph of \(y = f(x)\) for a function \(f\) defined on the interval \([a, b] = [0.5, 3.5]\). The Intermediate Value Theorem tells us that for every value of \(y\) between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\), there exists at least one value of \(x\) such that \(f(x) = y\).

You can see this on the picture below by sliding the blue point up and down the \(y\)-axis (click and drag the blue point with your mouse). This will move the horizontal line up and down. Let \(c\) be the \(y\)-coordinate of the blue point. You can then see visually that whenever \(c\) is between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\) on the \(y\)-axis, then the horizontal line will intersect the graph of the function in at least one point. These intersection points correspond to solutions of \(f(x) = c\).
