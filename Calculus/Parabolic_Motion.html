Velocity and acceleration for parabolic motion

Back to Calculus homepage


The following picture shows a point moving along the parabolic arc \(y = 1 - x^2\). The parametric equations of the point are \( (x(t), y(t)) = (t, 1-t^2) \). The velocity vector is then \( (x'(t), y'(t)) = (1, -2t) \) and the acceleration vector is \( (x''(t), y''(t)) = (0, -2) \).

You can see the velocity and acceleration on the picture below. At each point the velocity is tangent to the curve (this is true for any parametric curve). A feature of parabolic motion is that the acceleration vector is constant and always points directly downwards (for example, if you throw a ball through the air, then its trajectory is always a parabola (if we ignore air resistance), since the gravitational force is constant and always points downwards).
